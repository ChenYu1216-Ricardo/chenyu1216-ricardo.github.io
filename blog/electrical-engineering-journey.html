Beyond the Circuit: My Path in Electrical Engineering

From a curious high-school student to a UNSW tutor shaping the next generation of engineers.

Posted April 9, 2026· By Yu Chen· Engineering

There is a particular kind of magic that happens the moment a circuit you have designed springs to life for the first time — when an LED glows, a motor spins, or a waveform appears on an oscilloscope exactly as your calculations predicted. That feeling is what drew me to Electrical Engineering, and it is what continues to drive me every day at UNSW Sydney.

Caption: Debugging a waveform in the UNSW electrical engineering lab — every blip on the screen tells a story.

Growing up in Shandong, China, I was the kind of student who spent lunch breaks flipping through physics textbooks just for fun. Mathematics and science came naturally to me, but it was electricity — invisible, fast, and governing everything from smartphones to power grids — that captured my imagination most deeply. When the time came to choose a university program, Electrical Engineering at UNSW was an easy decision.

Why Electrical Engineering?

People sometimes ask whether I ever considered switching to software or finance, where job prospects are loudly advertised. My honest answer is no. Electrical Engineering sits at the intersection of the physical and the digital — it is one of the few disciplines where you design the hardware reality that all software ultimately runs on. That breadth is what makes it endlessly exciting.

At UNSW, the program challenged me to think across multiple abstraction layers simultaneously. In the same week, I might be solving differential equations that model an RLC circuit, writing assembly code for a microcontroller, and studying the quantum mechanics behind semiconductor devices. Each layer deepens your respect for the layers below it.

  • Circuit Analysis: Kirchhoff's laws, Thevenin/Norton equivalents, and transient response — the foundation everything else rests on.
  • Signal Processing: Fourier transforms, filters, and the elegant mathematics that lets us extract meaning from noise.
  • Intelligent Systems: Where classical control theory meets modern machine learning — my current area of deepest interest.
  • Power Electronics: Converting and distributing energy efficiently, which is increasingly critical in a world moving toward renewables.

Academic Highlights

I will not pretend grades are everything — some of my richest learning happened during failed experiments and late-night debugging sessions. But consistent academic performance does reflect the commitment I bring to the craft.

Throughout my degree, I have maintained a High Distinction (HD) Weighted Average Mark (WAM), placing me consistently among the top students in my cohort. Before arriving in Australia, I sat the Comprehensive Evaluation at Harbin Institute of Technology, where I achieved top scores that opened the door to international study pathways. I also accumulated national-level awards in physics and mathematics competitions back in Shandong — early signals of a mind that genuinely enjoys hard technical problems.

Caption: National competition recognition in physics and mathematics — proof that curiosity, applied consistently, compounds over time.

But transcripts only capture part of the picture. What they cannot show is the 2 a.m. moment when a circuit simulation finally converges, or the collaborative energy in a lab when five students collectively crack a problem no one could solve individually. Those experiences are the real education.

Becoming a Tutor: Teaching as the Deepest Form of Learning

In mid-2026, I joined the UNSW School of Electrical Engineering and Telecommunications as a Casual Academic tutor for DPST1081 — Electrical Engineering, a foundational first-year course that introduces students to circuit fundamentals, lab techniques, and engineering problem-solving.

Taking on this role was one of the best decisions I have made. There is a huge difference between knowing something and being able to explain it clearly to someone encountering it for the first time. When a first-year student looks confused about why current through an inductor cannot change instantaneously, you are forced to find multiple framings of the same idea until one clicks. That process sharpens your own understanding immeasurably.

What I love most about tutoring is watching the exact moment a concept lands — the slight widening of the eyes, the quick nod, the follow-up question that shows real engagement. Education, at its best, is a two-way transaction. My students teach me as much as I teach them.

  • Lab Guidance: Helping students build intuition for reading oscilloscopes, multimeters, and function generators — tools that no simulation fully replicates.
  • Problem-Solving Sessions: Walking through circuit analysis problems step by step, emphasising why each method works rather than just how.
  • Exam Preparation: Running revision workshops that focus on common misconceptions and high-yield problem types.

Future Aspirations

Engineering has never been, for me, purely about hardware. The projects I find most compelling are those where technical skill meets human need — where a well-designed system meaningfully improves someone's life.

In the near term, I am exploring the intersection of power electronics and sustainable energy: how smarter inverter designs and battery management systems can make renewable energy cheaper and more reliable at scale. Longer term, I am drawn to embedded intelligence — building systems that sense, decide, and act autonomously in the physical world, from agricultural monitoring networks to assistive medical devices.

I also intend to remain connected to education. Whether formally in an academic role, or informally through mentorship and open-source projects, I believe that sharing knowledge is one of the highest-leverage things an engineer can do. The best technology is not locked in a single mind; it spreads.

💡 Note If you are a first-year Electrical Engineering student feeling overwhelmed by circuit analysis, know this: confusion is a sign you are at the edge of your understanding, which is exactly where learning happens. Sit with the discomfort a little longer — the clarity usually follows.
